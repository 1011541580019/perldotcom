David Berk of Paramount Digital Entertainment is in charge of producing high traffic sites such as Star Trek, Entertainment Tonight Online. In this interview conducted by Tony Darugar of Binary Evolution, he talks about how and why Perl is used on their web sites. He also talks about using Binary Evolution's Velocigen product to speed-up the CGI systems used on these dynamic sites.

David, can you tell us about yourself, what you do, and what web sites you manage?

               Currently I'm Director of Technology at [Paramount Pictures New Media Group,] Paramount Digital Entertainment, primarily in charge of the internet impetus.

Our 3 largest properties are the Star Trek site, Entertainment Tonight Online, and the Entertainment Channel on netcenter, which is of course netscape.com.

We also have several smaller sites, mainly Paramount properties for the TV shows and movies, including Clueless, 7 days, Late Line, The Howie Mandel Show, Real TV, Frasier, and others.

Could you give us a sense of how much traffic these sites get?

               Our highest traffic site by far is Star Trek. It consists of a couple of different components:

  • The Continuum, which is the fan site, with chat, lots of information about the TV show, episode guides, how to speak Klingon, and other fun things.
  • The Star Trek store, which is our e-commerce component, where licensed merchandise can be purchased online.
  • Star Trek mail, a web based free email with Star Trek branding.

All told, this site pushes well over 5 million hits per day, a lot of it quite intensive, and much of it cannot be represented in hits (eg. chat, etc).

Netcenter also gets a lot of hits, because it's a channel on one of the larger portals.

The Entertainment Tonight site also drives a good amount of traffic, as it is very closely tied to the TV show.

I understand you are a big fan of Perl. Can you tell us why?

              

There are a couple of reasons.

First off, Perl makes it very easy to do what you need to get done. Perl does nearly everything, and also makes it easy to do the task at hand.

Also, it is rather easy to find and get Perl expertise, as opposed to some of the other development products, such as ASP, Cold Fusion, or Dynamo, where there is not nearly the sort of technical expertise available as there is for Perl.

Perl is easy to develop in, gets the task done, is fast, and chances are if there's something to be done, someone has already done it for you.

What about performance? A lot of people are concerned about performance when they use Perl on their web sites.

              

Performance is a big issue with us. In running a high volume, high availability entertainment presence, we have to be very cognizant of the brand. The Star Trek brand is a very powerful brand, and we cannot have anything on the site that would detract from it. Star Trek implies high technology: things that work. We cannot implement something that would either not work, or be slow. Because we push a lot of volume, performance is our number one concern.

Perl is, as you know, an interpreted language. The very nature of any CGI programming is that there is a lot of system overhead and bookkeeping with fork and exec, and doing a lot of the internal setup before you even do any of the actual work of the program.

One of the advantages to Binary Evolution's system is that it's sort of embedded: it embeds the Perl interpreter into the server process. So you see several advantages: you can maintain persistent information. Many of our sites maintain a lot of the information in an Oracle Database. With VelociGen, the database connections don't have to be created and torn down each time someone does something; they can be maintained in a pool and are available when needed. Also, we don't have to do a fork and exec, as you would when writing CGI's. A lot of the first pass processing is done for you, so really the only thing the CPU has to do is the meat of your progam. This saves us a lot of time, and saves us money, and we don't need as much hardware to do the same amount of stuff.

Scripting languages are often touted as decreasing application development time. Does Perl play a part in helping you develop applications quickly?

              

Oh, absolutely. Like any entertainment site, we have a very broad mandate in what we're responsible for. However, more often than not, we are viewed as a cost center, as opposed to a profit center, which means our budget can be severely limited at times.

Perl makes the cost of development fit within the budget. The development time in making value added enhancements around the margins is very quick for solving the problems at hand, and also it's very easy to maintain programs written in Perl because there is a lot of Perl expertise available.

Are there any other thoughts you want to leave us with?

               Sure. The thing we care about most is performance, performance, performance. The Star Trek site runs on 4 rather powerful servers with some load balancing hardware sitting in front of it. We noticed that as we tweak performance, the amount of traffic goes up, which means we are not necessarily limited by an interest in the site, or the amount of content that's available, but rather that the faster we can serve pages, the more pages we serve up.

So by getting performance out of the site, we increase, in essence, our revenue, because our revenue is tied directly to traffic. So, rather than buying more hardware, if we can do it with a software solution, which costs a fraction of the price, it's better for us.
